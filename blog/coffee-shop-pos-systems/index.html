Best POS Systems for Coffee Shops in 2026

Square, Toast, Clover, Lightspeed, and the specialty options — real fees, real features, and how to pick the right one for your concept.

The POS system you pick will handle every transaction, tip, refund, and report in your coffee shop for the next several years. Switching later is painful — staff retraining, data migration, new hardware, new integrations. Getting the choice right at the start saves thousands of dollars and dozens of hours over the lease term.

This is the 2026 comparison of the POS systems specialty coffee shops actually use, with real published pricing, the features that matter most for coffee operations, and the specific trade-offs that make each one right for certain concepts. Verify current pricing directly with each vendor — POS pricing changes often.

What a Good Coffee Shop POS Does

Before comparing vendors, a quick check on features that matter. A solid coffee shop POS should handle: fast order entry with customizable modifiers (milk types, flavor syrups, extra shots, temperature), integrated payment processing, tip prompts (fixed-percent and custom), loyalty program integration, mobile ordering and online ordering, inventory tracking (at least basic), staff management with time-clock, end-of-day reporting, and a customer-facing display for the order and tip prompt.

For shops that want to grow: multi-location reporting, wholesale/retail coffee bean sales with SKU-level inventory, gift card support, and an open API for integrations with accounting (QuickBooks, Xero), scheduling (7shots, Homebase), and marketing (Mailchimp, Klaviyo).

Square for Restaurants: The Default for Small Shops

Pricing (2026): Free tier for basic Square POS; Square for Restaurants Plus is $69/month per location; Premium is custom pricing. Card processing: 2.6% + 10¢ for in-person tap/dip/swipe as of 2026. Online orders: 2.9% + 30¢. Hardware: Square Register or iPad-based; Square Stand starts around $149.

Best for: Small independent shops opening for the first time, pop-ups, kiosks, and owners prioritizing fast setup. Square's restaurant-specific product handles modifiers, open tabs, and tip flow cleanly. Integrated payroll, online ordering, and Square Loyalty work out of the box.

Weaknesses: Card processing is higher than negotiated rates on Toast or Clover at volume. Inventory is functional but basic for bean retail. Custom development is limited.

Toast: The Restaurant Specialist

Pricing (2026): Starter plan is often $0/month for software but includes higher processing rates; Point of Sale plan is $69/month; Essentials bundles run $165-$299/month. Card processing is negotiated per merchant — typically 2.4% + 15¢ to 2.69% + 15¢ for in-person. Hardware is leased or purchased, typically $500-$1,500 up front per terminal.

Best for: Full-service concepts, shops with meaningful food programs, and shops planning to grow to multiple locations. Toast's kitchen display systems, online ordering, and back-office reporting are built for food service and work especially well for concepts that mix coffee + food + alcohol.

Weaknesses: Contract-heavy. Hardware is proprietary and Toast-branded. Pricing can balloon with add-ons. For a simple bar-only specialty shop, Toast is more capability than you need.

Clover: Flexible and Widely Supported

Pricing (2026): Software plans range from $14.95/month (Starter) to $84.90/month (Counter Service Restaurant). Card processing is through a bank or processor, not Clover directly — rates vary by processor but typically 2.3-2.7% + 10-15¢ in-person. Hardware: Clover Mini around $749, Clover Station Solo around $1,699, Clover Flex around $599.

Best for: Operators who want flexibility in payment processors and whose banks offer bundled Clover plans. The Clover app market is large, with specialty coffee apps for wholesale, bean subscriptions, and inventory.

Weaknesses: User experience is less polished than Square or Toast for restaurant workflows. Processor lock-in can happen depending on who sold you the system. Contract terms vary significantly.

Lightspeed Restaurant: Strong Reporting, International Support

Pricing (2026): Essentials plan around $69/month; Plus around $189/month; Pro around $399/month. Card processing through Lightspeed Payments, typically 2.6% + 10¢ in-person. Hardware: iPad-based, $300-$1,500 per terminal depending on configuration.

Best for: Multi-location shops, international operations, retailers combining bean sales and café. Lightspeed's inventory management and reporting tools are stronger than most competitors at the mid-tier, and Lightspeed Retail (for bean shops or combined retail + café) integrates tightly.

Weaknesses: Learning curve is steeper than Square. Overkill for single-location concepts.

SpotOn: Growing Challenger with Strong Loyalty

Pricing (2026): Restaurant plans starting around $99/month; custom pricing for larger operations. Card processing rates are negotiated. Hardware provided as part of the deal.

Best for: Shops that want integrated loyalty, marketing, and reservations in one platform. SpotOn's strength is the marketing stack — review management, email campaigns, loyalty — baked in rather than bolted on.

Coffee-Specific POS Options

Odeko (formerly Cloosiv): Coffee-specific mobile ordering layered on top of Square or a standalone POS. Strong at mobile ordering and the built-in loyalty/customer experience for repeat café customers. Often used alongside Square or Toast rather than as a standalone POS.

Joe Coffee App / Joe Coffee Pro: An independent-coffee-focused mobile ordering and loyalty platform. Less about being your POS and more about being your customer app.

How to Actually Compare: Total Cost of Ownership

The sticker price on the software monthly fee is a minor line. The real cost is the total cost of ownership: software + hardware + card processing + add-ons + switching cost.

Build a 3-year comparison. Assume $400,000 annual revenue and 95% card transactions. A 0.2% difference in card processing rate (e.g., 2.5% vs 2.7%) equals $760/year or $2,280 over three years — meaningful, but probably not decisive. A $60/month difference in software is $2,160 over three years. Hardware costs are generally one-time and amortize out.

Run the actual math. The POS that saves 0.3% on processing but adds $100/month in software can end up costlier than the one with higher processing rates and lower subscriptions.

Integrations Matter More Than Features

What separates a mediocre POS from a great one is usually integrations — what else the system connects to. Check specifically: accounting (QuickBooks Online, Xero), payroll (Gusto, ADP, Square Payroll), scheduling (7shifts, Homebase, When I Work), online ordering (is it native or does it require an add-on?), inventory (does it talk to your bean roaster's ordering portal?), marketing (Mailchimp, Klaviyo, SMS marketing), gift cards, delivery marketplaces (DoorDash, Uber Eats, Grubhub).

A POS with 80% of the features you want and 100% of the integrations will save more labor than a POS with 100% of the features and only 60% of the integrations.

The Contract Questions to Ask Before Signing

Beware of long-term contracts, especially with aggressive early-termination fees. Ask specifically: What's the contract length? Is there a month-to-month option? What's the early termination fee? Is hardware owned or leased? What's the card processing rate in writing (not verbal)? Is the rate locked or can it change? What's the chargeback fee? What happens to my data if I switch?

Verbal commitments from salespeople mean nothing. Everything goes in the contract or it doesn't exist.

Our Recommendation for Most New Shops

For a first-time independent specialty coffee shop under $500K in projected revenue: Square for Restaurants is usually the right default. Fast setup, no long contracts, solid feature set for coffee, integrates with most tools, and the pricing is transparent. You can switch later if you outgrow it — and by the time you do, you'll know exactly what you need in the next system.

For a shop with significant food operations or multi-location plans from day one: Toast is worth the contract for the restaurant-specific tooling.

For a shop combining retail bean sales with a café: Lightspeed shines because the retail + restaurant combo is handled in one system.

POS Is a Decision That Compounds

You'll make 100,000+ transactions through this system in your first three years. A POS that slows the line by two seconds per transaction — because of a clunky modifier screen or a slow printer — costs you 55+ hours of labor across that period. A POS whose reports don't give you the numbers you need for weekly review costs you in unseen ways even bigger. Pick carefully. Negotiate the contract. And budget the time to set it up right before opening day — not during morning rush in week one.

Set It Up Like the Line Depends On It (It Does)

Pre-opening POS setup is a multi-day task, not a one-hour exercise. Build out every drink, every size, every modifier, every food item, every tax rate, every discount code. Then test each button combination at the register before training the team on it. Mis-entered menus are the #1 source of transaction errors in week one, and transaction errors slow the line more than any other variable. Budget 15-25 hours of dedicated menu-building, tax-setup, and printer/cash drawer testing before training baristas. Then train for at least 3 hours specifically on the POS before you let anyone take a live customer order. The POS is the choke point of the shop's first day; if it's clean, the first day feels competent. If it's messy, every other system has to compensate.
