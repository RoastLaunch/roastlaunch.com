Coffee Shop Daily Operations: A Complete Routine for New Owners

Open, rush, mid-shift, close — the daily rhythm that keeps quality high, the team calm, and the numbers accurate.

A coffee shop that runs well runs on routine. Not rigid, not bureaucratic — just a consistent shape to the day that every team member can follow without thinking. The shops that feel calm during Saturday morning rush, the ones where the line moves and the espresso stays dialed and the pastry case looks full right up to close, are the shops where the operations routine is actually written down and actually followed.

This is a practical walkthrough of a coffee shop day — what happens at open, how to handle rush, what mid-shift looks like, how to close cleanly. Adapt to your volume and concept, but keep the structure. A documented daily operations plan is one of the highest-leverage things a new owner can put in place.

The Opening Shift: 90 Minutes Before Doors Unlock

The opening shift is the most important shift of the day. Done well, the rest of the day flows. Done poorly, the team spends the whole morning catching up.

60-90 minutes before open: Unlock, disarm alarm. Turn on espresso machine and grinders (many take 20-45 minutes to warm up properly and dial in). Turn on batch brewer, pastry case, ice machine if nightly shutdown. Check refrigeration temps and log.

45-60 minutes before open: Sanitizer mixed and tested. Hand-wash sinks stocked (soap, paper towels, hot water). Bar setup: grinders dialed in with fresh espresso pucks, milk pitchers staged, syrups topped off and dated, cups/lids/sleeves stocked to par. Bar surfaces sanitized.

30 minutes before open: Pull first espresso shots to dial in. Pour first batch brew. Taste both. Adjust grind if shots are running fast or slow. Brew times should fall in a 25-32 second window for a double shot; batch brew should taste balanced and not astringent.

15 minutes before open: Receive pastry delivery or pull baked goods. Plate and stage display case. Verify menu boards accurate. Music on at appropriate volume. Lighting set. Final walkthrough: customer-facing counter clean, floor free of debris, bathroom stocked and clean, outdoor seating wiped down if applicable.

At open: Unlock front door. Take a breath. First customer through the door starts the day.

Morning Rush: The 90-Minute Engine of the Business

Most shops do 40-60 percent of daily revenue between 7:00 and 10:30 a.m. The rush is what pays the rent. A well-run rush has three roles clearly separated, every time.

Cashier / Order Taker: Greets customer, takes order, inputs into POS, takes payment, calls drink back to the barista. If the line gets long, the cashier also manages the queue — eye contact, "I'll be right with you," smile. Cashier never makes drinks during peak.

Barista / Drink Maker: Pulls all espresso shots, steams all milk, assembles and hands off drinks. Calls drinks clearly as they're completed. Doesn't take orders, doesn't ring up transactions, doesn't touch cash.

Support / Floater (third person if volume supports): Restocks cups, refills milk pitchers from the reach-in, manages pastries, keeps the bar clean as drinks are made, runs food out to tables if applicable. The support role prevents the barista from ever stopping to grab a stray gallon of milk.

The three-role structure handles roughly 180-240 drinks per hour smoothly. Below that volume, two people suffice. Above that, add a second cashier or second barista.

Morning rush KPIs to watch: average ticket time (order received to drink in customer's hand — target under 4 minutes), wait time at peak (under 6 minutes), and any customer who leaves without ordering (this should be logged and addressed).

Mid-Shift: The Reset

Rush ends. The team takes a breath. And then mid-shift mistakes happen — because the bar looks clean enough and the next rush (lunch) seems far away. Don't let the team relax past the reset.

Mid-shift reset (10:30 a.m. or after rush subsides): Sanitize bar top, steam wand, portafilter baskets. Refill cups, lids, sleeves to par. Top off syrups. Replace milk pitchers with clean ones. Refill batch brew. Re-dial espresso if it's drifted (grind often needs a small adjustment as the bean temperature settles during the day). Wipe down dine-in tables and bathroom. Empty front trash if it's 80% full.

Mid-shift inventory check: How much milk left? Pastry case status? Any item approaching zero? If milk is at 30% of the day's start, order for tomorrow before 2 p.m. to hit the delivery cutoff. Don't wait to close to discover you're out of oat milk for the morning.

Staff breaks: 30-minute meal breaks and 15-minute rest breaks per labor law in your state. Schedule and take them. Burned-out staff after 4 hours without a break is a quality and retention problem.

Afternoon: The Different Business

Afternoon (12-4 p.m.) is a different customer than morning. More seated customers, more laptops, more chats, more iced drinks, more retail bag pickups. The operational focus shifts from speed to hospitality.

Keep the floor clean. Wipe tables between customers. Check bathroom every hour. Refill sugar/stir stations. Walk the floor — say hello to regulars, ask if anyone needs anything. Afternoon is when relationships with customers are built.

Afternoon is also when the bar should get a deeper mid-day cleaning: group heads cleaned, steam wand tips cleared, blades of the grinder cleared if needed, floors behind the bar swept and mopped.

Closing Shift: 90 Minutes After Last Call

Close is where shortcuts become long-term problems. A sloppy close leaves the opening team behind schedule and the shop in worse shape each day. A disciplined close takes 60-90 minutes and resets everything for tomorrow.

30 minutes before close: Stop accepting drip refills past final pot. Begin cleaning stations not currently in active use. Break down unused pastries (if your protocol is to discard rather than carry over).

At close: Lock front door. Turn over sign. Last customers finish. Thank them as they leave.

Closing procedures (in order): Count cash drawers and reconcile against POS. Run end-of-day reports: gross sales, category breakdown, labor, voids. Break down espresso machine: backflush with cleaner (Cafiza/Puly Caff), soak portafilters and baskets, wipe steam wand tips, clean drip trays. Empty and clean milk pitchers. Drain and rinse grinders. Clean pour-over stations. Drain and rinse drip brewer.

Dish pit: wash, sanitize, and air-dry everything. Three-compartment sink drained and wiped. Pastry case emptied, wiped with sanitizer.

Front: wipe all tables, chairs pushed in or stacked. Sweep and mop. Take out trash. Clean bathroom thoroughly. Menu boards updated if pricing or offerings changed.

Close paperwork: count cash drawer to bank deposit envelope, lock in safe or deposit overnight. Close cash register. Email or save end-of-day report.

Final walkthrough: refrigeration temps rechecked and logged. Equipment powered down per protocol. Alarm set. Doors locked.

Total close: 75-90 minutes with a team of two. A close that takes 2+ hours consistently means the during-day routine isn't holding up.

Weekly Rhythm

Layered on top of daily routine is the weekly rhythm of deeper work:

Sunday (or Monday morning): Weekly inventory count. Weekly order placed. Waste log reviewed. Schedule confirmed for the coming week.

Mid-week (Wednesday): Deep clean: scrub floor drains, deep-clean dishwasher, descale ice machine filters, deep-clean pastry case. Team meeting — 15-minute huddle on what's working and what's not.

End of week (Friday or Saturday morning): Espresso machine deep clean: remove group head screens, soak, clean, reassemble. Grinder cleaning with Grindz or equivalent.

Monthly: Full equipment cleaning, professional machine PM (preventive maintenance) scheduled, financial close, category review.

Documentation: The Thing That Makes It Transferable

Every procedure in this article should be written down in a Standard Operating Procedures (SOP) document — a living binder or digital doc that every employee can reference. Laminated open/close checklists posted at the bar. Recipe cards for every menu item with exact weights, times, and temperatures. A cleaning schedule matrix (daily, weekly, monthly tasks by role). An emergency procedures guide (power out, water out, broken equipment).

The SOPs aren't bureaucracy. They're the system that lets you take a day off without the shop falling apart. They're how new hires ramp up in a week instead of a month. They're the operational asset that — eventually — makes the shop sellable as a business, not just a job you own.

Routine Creates Freedom

It feels counterintuitive that the path to a calmer, happier shop runs through more structure, more checklists, and tighter routines. But it's true. When everyone knows what to do at each moment of the day, the shop stops running on the owner's energy and starts running on its own systems. That's when the owner gets a real day off, the team feels confident, and the business becomes something that lasts.

The Weekly Reset

Beyond daily routines, build one dedicated hour each week — Monday morning is common — when the owner or manager resets the operation for the week ahead. Review last week's numbers (sales, labor %, food cost %). Confirm this week's schedule and any gaps. Check inventory status and place orders. Walk the shop and note any maintenance issues. Read the team's shift notes from the past week and respond to any open items. One focused hour per week compounds over time into a shop that feels under control rather than perpetually reactive. Skip it and the small issues pile up into big ones.
