The First 90 Days After Opening Your Coffee Shop

The weekly priorities, the numbers to watch, and the habits that turn a chaotic opening into a stable business.

Opening day is the easy part. The first 90 days after opening is where most new coffee shops reveal their real character — or discover fatal flaws. Revenue stabilizes (or doesn't). Team turnover hits (or doesn't). Operational issues compound (or get solved). The buzz fades and you're left with the actual business, running at actual volume, with actual problems you didn't see during the build-out.

This is the guide to the first 90 days. What to focus on each month, what numbers to watch weekly, what to fix in what order, and what habits to install now so the business has a chance of being worth owning in year two. Most of this is not glamorous. All of it is essential.

Month One: Stabilize the Operation

Month one is survival mode. Don't try to optimize, expand, or grow. Get the shop to a place where it operates without you in the building for 4 hours.

Week 1-2 priority: quality consistency. Every drink tastes the same, every time. Espresso is dialed morning and afternoon. Milk temperature is right. Pastries are fresh. The bar is clean. Skip new menu items, special events, catering inquiries — anything that pulls focus from the core 20 drinks you serve every day.

Week 2-3 priority: the opening and closing routine. Every open and close runs on a written checklist. Every opener knows what to do, in what order. Every closer does the same close in 75-90 minutes. No "we'll figure it out today" openings or "just do your best" closes. Document, tweak, document, tweak.

Week 3-4 priority: the team. Who can run the bar solo? Who can open alone? Who can close alone? Who can't yet but should be able to by day 60? By the end of month one, you should have one shift lead besides yourself who can handle a full shift competently.

Numbers to watch weekly (month one):

• Daily transaction count — trending up or flat?

• Average ticket — at or above projection?

• Email list growth — at least 100/week?

• Google reviews — count and average (target: 50+ reviews at 4.7+ stars by end of month three)

• Staff mood — who's happy, who's struggling?

Month Two: Find the Operational Leaks

By day 30, the shop is running. Volume has settled. The initial chaos has resolved. Now the question is: where is the money leaking out?

Weekly P&L review: Get your bookkeeper or accountant to produce a weekly P&L. Compare to projections. Where are you above and below? The categories that most often come in worse than projected in month two: labor (too many hours scheduled "just in case"), COGS (waste, portioning drift, inventory shrinkage), credit card processing (actual rate higher than quoted), and utilities (especially if the shop runs HVAC inefficiently).

Inventory audit: First full monthly inventory count. Compare theoretical COGS (what you should have used based on sales) to actual COGS (what you really used). Variance above 3% means something is leaking — usually over-portioning, waste you're not logging, or staff meals not tracked.

Labor audit: Actual labor hours vs scheduled. Actual labor as a percent of revenue — target 28-33%. If labor is running 38%+, trim scheduled hours during slow periods, push back start times, or reorganize the bar flow so fewer people handle the same volume.

Customer data: Who are your regulars? How often do they come? What do they buy? Most POS systems can answer this. A shop with 80+ regulars who come 3+ times per week has a foundation. A shop with 20 regulars and high one-time traffic has a retention problem.

Operational issues to tackle in month two: the one broken piece of equipment that still isn't fixed, the vendor who keeps short-shipping, the POS feature that's slowing transactions, the cleaning task that keeps getting missed. Fix them systematically. Don't let issues accrue into month three.

The "What's Working, What's Not" Review at Day 60

At exactly 60 days in, sit down with your numbers and write two honest lists. What's working — customers love it, revenue supports it, team executes it? What's not — complaints, low attach rates, wasted inventory, team friction?

Common month-two findings:

What's often working: core 5-10 menu drinks. Morning rush flow. Customer experience in low-volume times. The shop's atmosphere. Specific team members who are exceeding expectations.

What's often not: specific menu items that aren't selling (cut them). A complex modifier structure that slows the line (simplify). The open shift with weak staffing (rebalance schedules). Afternoon volume below projections (add an afternoon promotion or rethink afternoon staffing). Pastries you're throwing away daily (reduce order quantity or discontinue).

Make specific decisions off the review. Don't just note the issues — execute changes in week 9-10.

Month Three: Build the Marketing Engine

Months one and two are operational. Month three is when marketing deserves real attention.

Instagram discipline: Commit to 3-4 posts per week, minimum. Half behind-the-scenes (the team, the prep, the process), half customer-facing (drinks, food, atmosphere). Stories daily when you can. If you can't post yourself, hire a local photographer or social media helper for $200-$500/month.

Email campaigns: Bi-weekly email to your list. Content: new menu items, seasonal changes, behind-the-scenes stories, customer spotlights, events. Average open rate target: 30%+. Click rate: 3%+. A healthy list of 1,500 driving 10% of that into the shop means 150 visits per email — meaningful revenue.

Google My Business: Keep photos fresh (add 3-5 per month). Respond to every review — positive and negative — within 48 hours. Post weekly updates. Google My Business is one of the highest-ROI marketing channels for local retail and most shops neglect it.

Neighborhood partnerships: The partnerships you started pre-opening should be deepening. Cross-promotions. Joint events. Shared email lists. The goal is weaving the shop into the fabric of the neighborhood so that you're the default recommendation.

Local press follow-up: Any press that mentioned you at opening — thank them, share the coverage on Instagram, tag them. Keep the relationship warm for when you have more news (a one-year anniversary, a new coffee origin, a charity event).

The 90-Day Financial Review

At day 90, sit with your accountant and look at the full picture. Three months of actual P&L. Actual revenue trend. Actual expense categories. Actual margins. Compare to pre-opening projections.

Key questions to answer:

Is revenue on a trajectory to hit the year-one target? Most successful shops grow revenue 5-15% per month for the first 6-9 months, then plateau. If you're flat or declining after 3 months, the concept-location-execution fit needs honest diagnosis.

Is the business on track to be cash-flow positive by month 12? Most shops need 6-18 months. If your trajectory shows cash flow positive never, you have a structural problem — usually rent too high, labor too heavy, or pricing too low.

What's the runway on your working capital? If you opened with $60K in reserve and you've burned $30K in the first 90 days, you have 6 more months before the reserve is gone. Plan accordingly. You may need to reduce burn or secure additional capital before month 6.

What's the COGS line looking like? Target 28-32%. If it's 36%+, fix urgently — pricing, portioning, waste, supplier negotiation.

What's the labor line? Target 28-33%. If it's 38%+, rebalance.

Habits That Carry Into Year One

The first 90 days is the time to install the habits that turn running-the-shop from a panic into a system.

Weekly Monday review. Ninety minutes every Monday morning. Review the prior week's numbers (revenue, transactions, COGS, labor). Review the week's schedule and orders. Plan the week's marketing. Write three priorities for the week. Every successful small business owner has this rhythm or its equivalent.

Monthly financial close. First week of every month, close the prior month's books with your accountant. Review the P&L against budget. Spot drift early — a 2% increase in COGS or a 4% increase in labor, caught this month, is a problem you can fix. Caught three months from now, it's a crisis.

Quarterly strategic review. Every three months, zoom out. What's working? What's not? What's the next quarter's priority? What's one thing to stop doing, one thing to start, one thing to keep?

Personal discipline: One day off per week, minimum. Burnout is the silent killer of year-one owners. The shop that lasts is the one with an owner who's still sane at month 12.

The 90-Day Pivot — If You Need It

Some shops find in the first 90 days that the concept isn't working. Not enough volume. Wrong neighborhood mix. Wrong pricing. Unexpected competition. If the numbers clearly don't support the plan at day 60-90, you have more options than "push through and hope": adjust pricing, tighten the menu, rebalance the labor model, add a revenue stream (catering, wholesale bean sales, a lunch program), restructure vendor contracts. Most failing shops could have been saved with earlier, harder decisions at day 90.

Denial is the enemy. A shop pulling $300K when you projected $500K has a structural gap you need to close — not a "next month will be better" hope. Make the hard call when the data says to make it.

Survive the 90, Earn the Year

The first 90 days is when chaos becomes system, when optimism meets P&L, when the shop stops being an idea and becomes a business you actually have to run. Most shops that survive the first year do the work in those 90 days. Stabilize the operation. Find the leaks. Build the marketing engine. Install the habits. By day 90, you should know whether the business works — and if it does, you should be running it with a rhythm that can scale into years two, three, and beyond. The shop you have at day 90 is, with minor iteration, the shop you'll have at day 365. Make day 90 count.
