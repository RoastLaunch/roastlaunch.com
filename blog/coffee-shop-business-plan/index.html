Coffee Shop Business Plan: How to Write One That Actually Gets Funded

A coffee shop business plan isn't a school assignment. It's the document that convinces a lender you're a safe bet. Here's exactly what needs to be in it.

A coffee shop business plan isn't a creative writing project. It's a document with one job: convince a lender, an investor, or a serious partner that you've thought through every way the business could fail and built a real plan to get around each one. Lenders read dozens of restaurant and café plans a month. The ones that get funded look almost identical in structure. The ones that get rejected are usually rejected for the same handful of reasons.

This guide walks through the nine sections a bankable coffee shop business plan must contain, what each section actually needs to say, and the specific gaps that cause SBA lenders and commercial banks to pass. Written for independent specialty coffee shop founders — not for someone building a coffeehouse chain.

Who Actually Reads a Coffee Shop Business Plan

Before you write a single word, know your audience. A coffee shop business plan has roughly four readers, and each one weights the document differently.

A commercial bank or SBA lender reads it for default risk. They want to see historical data, realistic projections, and a debt-service coverage ratio above 1.25. They'll spend the most time on financials and the owner's personal financial statement.

An equity investor (uncommon for independent shops but possible) reads it for return potential. They want big market, clear differentiation, and a path to exit.

A landlord or commercial broker may ask for the plan before approving your lease. They want to see that you're not going to go out of business in year one and leave their space dark.

And you yourself should be the hardest reader. A plan that doesn't force you to confront the hardest questions — what happens in a slow quarter, what the actual customer count needs to be, whether you can make payroll in month four — isn't doing its job.

Section 1: Executive Summary

The executive summary is the one section every reader will actually read. It's also the section you should write last, because it's a compression of everything else. Keep it to one page or less.

A good executive summary tells the reader, in the first 100 words, what the shop is, where it's located, who it serves, what makes it different, how much capital you need, and what the one-line financial picture looks like. Example compressed version: "Ebenezer Coffee is a 1,100-square-foot specialty coffee shop opening in the Westwood neighborhood of Columbus, Ohio, serving locally-roasted single-origin espresso drinks and a house-made pastry program to the neighborhood's 14,500 households, of whom 38 percent are college-educated professionals aged 25 to 44. Requested financing is $185,000 via SBA 7(a) loan, with $55,000 in founder equity already secured. Year-one projected revenue: $410,000 at 17 percent net margin after the first six-month ramp."

Include the ask. Lenders want to see the specific dollar amount, the specific use of funds, and the specific term. Vague asks get passed.

Section 2: Company Description and Concept

What kind of coffee shop is this, specifically? "Community-focused specialty café" is not specific enough. Three descriptors that actually mean something: the shop's format (dine-in café, counter service, kiosk, drive-thru, hybrid), the shop's positioning (neighborhood third-place, productivity-focused work café, craft destination, commuter grab-and-go), and the shop's product focus (espresso-forward, filter-coffee-forward, coffee-and-food hybrid).

Then: what's the origin story, what's the ownership structure, and what's the legal entity. A single-member LLC taxed as a pass-through is the standard for a first shop. If you've elected to be taxed as an S-corp or C-corp, say why.

Section 3: Market Analysis

This is where most first-time plans fall apart. Lenders can tell in ten seconds whether a market analysis is generic boilerplate copied from a sample template, or whether the applicant has actually walked the trade area.

A credible market analysis includes the specific trade area (usually a 1 to 3 mile radius, or a 10 to 15 minute walking circle depending on density), the demographic profile of that trade area (census data by block group — not the whole city), foot traffic data if relevant, the competitive set with names and addresses, the broader coffee industry context, and — most importantly — the specific gap in the market your shop fills.

Industry-level context to include: the U.S. specialty coffee market reached $111.5 billion in 2025 and is projected to grow at roughly 10 percent annually through 2033. Forty-five percent of Americans now consume specialty coffee on any given day, up from 25 percent a decade ago. The independent café segment — not chains — captures a growing share as consumers increasingly prefer local, craft-focused operators.

Local-level data is what separates a real plan from a template. Pull block-group demographics, count existing coffee shops within your trade area, score each competitor on hours, positioning, and quality, and identify what's missing. "No specialty-grade espresso program within 1.2 miles" is a real insight. "There's room for more coffee shops" is not.

Section 4: Competitive Analysis

List every coffee-serving business within your trade area. Not just shops — gas stations, bagel places, bookstores with espresso machines, convenience stores with specialty coffee programs. Anyone selling a cup of coffee in your neighborhood competes for the same morning-routine decision.

For each direct competitor, capture: address and format, approximate seats, hours, approximate price point, product quality (go in, order, evaluate), and approximate customer mix. Then name, explicitly, what you do differently and why a rational customer would walk past a competitor to come to you. If you can't name a specific, defensible difference — quality, menu, location, hours, atmosphere — you don't yet have a business.

Section 5: Products and Services

This section describes the menu, the sourcing, and the service model in enough detail that a lender understands what you're selling. Menu categories with price ranges (espresso drinks $4 to $6, filter coffee $3.50 to $5, pastries $3 to $7, lunch items $9 to $14 if applicable). Coffee sourcing: the roaster or roasters you plan to partner with, whether beans are single-origin or blends, and the wholesale price per pound you're modeling into COGS. Milk program, pastry program (in-house vs. wholesale partner), retail program.

Lenders don't need every SKU. They need enough detail to validate that your gross margin assumptions are realistic.

Section 6: Marketing and Sales Strategy

How will people learn you exist? How will they become regulars? A coffee shop's marketing plan is usually less about paid advertising and more about the pre-launch runway, neighborhood relationships, and the systems that turn first-time customers into 3-times-a-week regulars.

Include: your pre-launch runway (90-day build of social presence and email list before open), soft-opening plan, grand-opening plan, ongoing marketing channels and approximate monthly budget, loyalty program structure, referral mechanisms, and community partnerships (local businesses, schools, churches). Most shops spend 2 to 5 percent of revenue on marketing in year one, dropping to 1 to 3 percent in steady state.

Section 7: Operations Plan

The day-to-day. Hours of operation, staffing model (opening and closing shifts, baristas-per-shift at peak), key vendors, equipment list with supplier, inventory management approach, food safety and health code compliance approach, opening timeline from lease signing to grand opening (typically 4 to 8 months).

Include the organizational chart even if it's just "founder-operator" in year one. Lenders want to see that you've thought through what happens when you're sick, when you need to hire, and how SOPs will be documented.

Section 8: Management Team and Ownership

Who's running this, and why are they qualified? If you have no coffee or hospitality experience, name that honestly and describe how you're closing the gap — barista training, owner mentorship, SCA certification, partnership with an experienced manager. Lenders prefer honesty paired with a plan over inflated résumés.

Include: your background and relevant experience, key advisors or mentors, planned hires (especially a strong lead barista or manager), and ownership percentages if there are multiple owners.

Section 9: Financial Projections

This section is what will actually get you funded or rejected. A bankable coffee shop business plan contains three financial documents at minimum, modeled out three to five years.

The Profit and Loss Statement (P&L): monthly for year one, quarterly for years two and three, annually for years four and five. Revenue broken out by category (espresso drinks, filter coffee, food, retail). COGS as a percentage of revenue (specialty coffee shops target 28 to 32 percent). Operating expenses: rent, labor (28 to 35 percent is the benchmark), marketing, utilities, repairs, insurance, supplies, professional services, bank and processing fees, loan payments. Net income.

The Cash Flow Statement: because a coffee shop can be profitable on paper and still run out of cash. Starting cash, monthly cash in, monthly cash out, loan draws, capital expenditures, ending cash. This is the document that exposes whether your working-capital buffer is large enough.

The Balance Sheet: assets (cash, equipment, inventory, deposits), liabilities (loans, accounts payable), and owner's equity.

Anchor every projection in a defensible assumption. Revenue should be built from transactions-per-day times average ticket times open days. Most independent specialty cafés in a walkable neighborhood do 120 to 250 transactions per day at an average ticket of $7 to $11 — giving year-one revenue of roughly $315,000 to $1,000,000 depending on trade area and maturity. Industry net margins for mature independent shops cluster around 10 to 25 percent; new shops in year one typically run 2 to 6 percent or post a small loss before reaching stabilized operations. Use the conservative end of those ranges in your plan, not the aspirational end. A lender who sees aggressive assumptions they can poke holes in will pass. A lender who sees conservative assumptions they can stress-test will lean in.

The Five Mistakes That Kill Plans

After reviewing industry data on SBA approval rates and the reasons independent coffee shop plans get declined, a pattern is obvious. Five failure modes account for the vast majority of rejections.

Aspirational revenue assumptions. Projecting $700,000 in year-one revenue when comparable shops in comparable markets do $350,000. Lenders know the benchmarks. If yours don't match, they assume you don't either.

No working-capital line. Building a budget that funds build-out and equipment but not the six months of runway before profitability. This is the single most common rejection reason.

Generic market analysis. Demographics pulled from the metro level instead of the block group, competitors listed without evaluation, no specific gap identified.

Missing or weak owner contribution. SBA programs typically expect the owner to contribute 10 to 20 percent of total project cost as equity. If you're bringing nothing, lenders assume you have no skin in the game.

Inconsistencies between sections. The operations plan says three employees; the P&L shows two. The market analysis says 220 transactions per day; the revenue build shows 150. Lenders read the whole plan looking for consistency. Gaps signal a founder who hasn't modeled their own business.

How Long Should It Be, and What Should It Look Like

Thirty to fifty pages including financial schedules is the standard range. Under 20 pages reads as unfinished; over 60 reads as padded. Use standard formatting — 11-point type, standard margins, a proper cover page, a one-page executive summary, a table of contents, clear section headers. Include your financial statements as appendices with the schedules running out five years.

Writing the plan will take 40 to 120 hours if you're doing it seriously. Pay for a professional financial model template or build your own in Excel — don't ballpark the projections. And before submitting, have at least two people read it: someone who has operated a café before, and someone who has submitted an SBA loan application before. They'll see gaps you can't.

A coffee shop business plan isn't the business. But a business that can't be credibly described in a business plan usually isn't a business either. The exercise of writing it forces the clarity that opening day will demand.
