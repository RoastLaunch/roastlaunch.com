How to Write a Coffee Shop Lease: The Terms That Actually Protect You

The lease clauses that decide whether you keep your business when something goes wrong — and the ones landlords bury that will quietly end you.

The lease is the single most important legal document you will ever sign for your coffee shop. It locks you into a five- to ten-year financial commitment that typically represents $200,000 to $600,000 in total payments — more than the entire cost of opening the business. And yet most first-time owners sign the landlord's draft with only surface edits, trusting the broker, trusting the handshake, trusting that the boilerplate is "standard."

It is not standard. Commercial leases are written by the landlord's attorney to favor the landlord. That's their job. Your job is to read every clause, push back on the ones that put you at risk, and walk away from any lease where the landlord won't negotiate the handful of terms that genuinely matter. This is the guide to what those terms are.

Start With the LOI, Not the Lease

Never start with the landlord's lease draft. Start with a Letter of Intent (LOI) — a 2-to-4 page summary of the deal you actually want. The LOI is non-binding but establishes the negotiated terms before either side invests in lawyers reviewing a 50-page document.

Your LOI should lay out: base rent per square foot, NNN treatment, term length, renewal options, tenant improvement allowance, free rent period, permitted use, exclusive use protection, personal guarantee terms, assignment rights, and any critical landlord obligations. Everything on the LOI should appear in the lease exactly as stated. If a clause shows up in the lease that wasn't in the LOI — or shows up in a weaker form — that's a red flag about the landlord's good faith.

Base Rent, NNN, and the True Cost of the Space

The "rent" you see in the listing is almost never the rent you pay. Most commercial leases in 2026 are "triple-net" (NNN), meaning on top of base rent you pay a pro-rata share of property taxes, property insurance, and common area maintenance (CAM). NNN charges typically add $4 to $20 per square foot per year to a lease — on a 1,000-square-foot space, that's an extra $4,000 to $20,000 annually above the sticker rent.

Before you sign, ask for the current year's actual NNN reconciliation, last year's reconciliation, and the estimate for the coming year. Ask what's included in CAM (landscaping? parking lot repaving? snow removal? management fees?). CAP the annual NNN increase at a number you can live with — 3 to 5 percent is a reasonable cap. Without a CAP, the landlord can raise NNN by any amount, and disputes over what qualifies as CAM are how honest small tenants get surprised.

Calculate your true rent: base rent + NNN + utilities + insurance. That's your occupancy cost. Keep it under 10 percent of projected gross revenue. Above 12 percent and the lease is at risk of sinking the shop.

Term Length and Option Structure

Landlords push for longer initial terms. Tenants should push for shorter initial terms with renewal options. The ideal structure for a new independent: five-year initial term with two five-year options to renew at a pre-negotiated rent escalation.

Five years is long enough that the landlord will invest in tenant improvement, but short enough that you aren't trapped in a failing location. The options give you control — you can stay if the business is working, walk if it isn't, and the landlord can't raise rent arbitrarily on renewal because the escalation formula is already in the lease. Typical option rent escalation: fixed 3 percent annual increases, or CPI with a 3 percent cap and 2 percent floor.

Beware ten- or fifteen-year initial terms without exit rights. In a new shop, year one to three is when most failures happen. A fifteen-year lease with no out turns a failed shop into personal bankruptcy.

Personal Guarantee: The Clause That Puts Your House at Risk

A personal guarantee makes you, individually, responsible for the full remaining rent if the LLC defaults. On a seven-year lease with $4,000/month rent, that's over $300,000 of personal exposure. Most new-tenant leases require a personal guarantee. The question is how to limit it.

Three structures to push for, in order of preference. First, no personal guarantee — rare for new operators without an operating history, but worth asking. Second, a "burn-down" or "good-guy" guarantee, where your personal liability decreases over time (e.g., reduces by 20 percent each year) or terminates entirely if you give proper notice and surrender the space clean and current on rent. Good-guy guarantees are the most common outcome for well-negotiated leases. Third, a capped guarantee limited to six to twelve months of rent, regardless of the lease term.

Never sign an unlimited full-term personal guarantee unless you can afford to write a six-figure personal check if the shop doesn't work. It's the single clause most likely to turn a business failure into a life-altering financial event.

Tenant Improvement Allowance (TI): Cash You Shouldn't Leave on the Table

TI allowance is money the landlord contributes toward your build-out. In 2026, TI ranges from $0 on short-term deals to $50+ per square foot on longer-term leases in competitive markets. On a 1,000-square-foot space at $30 per square foot, that's $30,000 the landlord gives you toward build-out.

Structure matters. Push for TI to be funded on completion (landlord reimburses you as build-out progresses) rather than as rent abatement. Push for TI to be amortized into rent only if you get additional free rent in exchange. Always specify exactly what TI can cover (plumbing, electrical, HVAC, flooring — yes; your espresso machine — no) and the documentation required to draw on it.

Free rent ("rent abatement") is another form of landlord contribution. Two to six months free rent at the start of the term is common in 2026 and helps cover the pre-opening period when you're paying contractors but not making revenue.

Permitted Use and Exclusive Use Clauses

The permitted use clause defines what you're allowed to sell and do in the space. Push for language broad enough to cover the business you might evolve into: "operation of a specialty coffee shop and café, including the sale of coffee beverages, tea, baked goods, breakfast and lunch food items, prepared food, retail coffee beans and merchandise, and related items." Narrow language can prevent you from adding hot food, selling wine and beer, or running a retail program later.

Even more important: exclusive use. An exclusive clause prevents the landlord from leasing space in the same center to a competitor. For a coffee shop, push for exclusivity on specialty coffee — "Landlord shall not lease any other space in the Property to any business whose primary purpose is the sale of coffee beverages." This prevents a Starbucks or another independent from opening 100 feet away and splitting your customer base. Landlords resist broad exclusivity but often grant a narrow version. Even a narrow version is worth tens of thousands of dollars over the lease term.

Assignment and Sublease: Your Exit Door

If the shop isn't working — or if it is working and someone wants to buy it — you need the right to transfer the lease. Landlords typically require their consent for assignment or sublease, but they should not be allowed to unreasonably withhold that consent.

Push for the language "not to be unreasonably withheld, conditioned, or delayed." Push for a specific time window (15 to 30 days) after which if the landlord hasn't responded, consent is deemed granted. Push for the right to assign to a well-capitalized buyer in the same industry without consent. This is the clause that determines whether you can sell the business, which is the primary way owners recoup their investment.

Repair, Maintenance, and the "AS-IS" Trap

Who fixes what, and who pays for it? In a true NNN lease, the tenant is often responsible for nearly everything — including HVAC, roof repairs, and structural issues. That can shift $20,000 to $100,000 of risk to you.

Negotiate the landlord's retained responsibilities clearly. Structural components (roof, foundation, exterior walls, load-bearing elements) should always remain the landlord's responsibility. HVAC is a common battleground — push for the landlord to warrant the HVAC system is in good working order at delivery, cover major HVAC replacement (anything over $2,500 in a single repair), and require a landlord-funded service contract for the first year. Without this, you can spend $8,000 in month four on a failing rooftop unit.

Also beware the "AS-IS" clause. If the landlord delivers the space "AS-IS," you accept it in whatever condition it's in — including code violations, undisclosed mechanical problems, and environmental issues. Push for a "delivery condition" clause specifying exactly what condition the space must be in at delivery (vanilla shell, broom-clean, HVAC operational, electrical to code, etc.).

Signage, Hours, and Operational Restrictions

Read the signage clause carefully. Some leases restrict sign size, illumination, exterior branding, and window graphics — any of which can hurt visibility for a coffee shop that lives or dies on street presence. Require specific signage rights, including building-mounted signage, window branding, and sandwich-board or A-frame rights for the sidewalk if applicable.

Operational restrictions also matter. Required operating hours ("you must be open 7 days, 6am-9pm") can force you into payroll you can't afford in year one. Radius restrictions (you can't open another shop within X miles) can block expansion. Push back on anything that limits operational flexibility unless the trade-off is substantial.

Use a Real Estate Attorney — No Exceptions

A commercial real estate attorney in 2026 typically charges $1,500 to $4,000 to review and negotiate a first lease. That cost is nothing compared to the protection it provides. Do not use your general-practice attorney. Do not skip this because the landlord's "standard form" looks reasonable. A specialist will spot the seven or eight material issues a generalist will miss — and will negotiate them in language that actually holds up.

Bring your attorney in after the LOI is agreed but before the lease is finalized. Pay them to redline the draft. Let them negotiate the legal language with landlord's counsel. You handle the business terms (rent, TI, term). They handle the legal language (indemnification, defaults, holdover rent, casualty, condemnation, subordination). This division of labor is how good deals get done.

The Lease Is the Business

The clauses in this article aren't exotic. Every commercial real estate attorney has negotiated every one of them a hundred times. What matters is that you know which ones are worth fighting for — because a landlord will never volunteer them, and a broker may not push on your behalf. Signing a clean lease is the most leveraged legal moment in the life of your business. Take the extra month. Pay the attorney. Redline every draft. The lease you sign determines whether the shop you build is an asset you own or a liability you rent.
