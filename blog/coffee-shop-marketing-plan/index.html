Coffee Shop Marketing Plan: A Complete 90-Day Guide for New Owners

Pre-launch buzz, grand opening, and the 90-day campaign cadence that builds a durable customer base without burning the budget.

A coffee shop marketing plan is not a 40-page document. It's a set of clear decisions: who you're trying to reach, what channels you'll use, how often you'll show up on each, and how you'll measure whether it's working. Done well, a marketing plan turns a new shop from "some coffee place on Elm" into a name the neighborhood knows within 90 days of opening. Done poorly — or not at all — the shop remains invisible and the owner spends a year wondering why foot traffic is soft.

This is the practical 90-day marketing plan for a new independent coffee shop in 2026. It's built for an owner with no marketing background, limited time, and a lean budget. The core principles: consistent presence beats clever campaigns; local beats viral; owned channels (email, Instagram, Google) beat paid ads for an independent at this scale.

The Four Channels That Matter Most

For an independent coffee shop, four channels do 90% of the marketing work:

Instagram. The shop's daily face to the market. Where discovery happens, where regulars check in, and where new customers decide if you're worth a visit.

Email list. The highest-ROI channel by far. Every shop should build one from day one.

Google My Business. The most neglected, highest-impact channel for local discovery. "Coffee shops near me" searches drive real traffic.

Word of mouth. Not a channel you control, but one you can feed. Every customer experience is either earning or losing you future customers.

A shop that executes these four well will outperform a shop that spreads thin across 10 channels. The mistake most new owners make is trying to be on TikTok, Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn, YouTube, Pinterest, and every other platform. Pick the few that matter and do them well.

Pre-Launch (60-30 Days Before Opening)

Marketing starts before the doors open. Specific priorities:

Establish Instagram presence. Create the account, design the bio, post 2-3 times per week. Content: behind-the-scenes of the build-out, the story of the shop, you personally, the coffee origin stories, what's coming. Follow every local business and neighborhood account. Engage generously on their content.

Build email list infrastructure. Set up Mailchimp or Klaviyo. Create a landing page with a sign-up form: "Be first in line at our opening — join the founding member list for early access + a free drink on opening week." Promote through Instagram, local partners, and anywhere you have exposure.

Claim Google My Business. Set up the listing with correct address, opening hours (even if "coming soon"), photos of the space, and the eventual menu. Verify the listing. This often takes 2-4 weeks to fully process.

Local partnerships. Identify 10-15 nearby businesses with aligned customers — yoga studios, gyms, boutiques, bookstores, coworking spaces. Reach out and propose a simple pre-opening coffee drop: you bring coffee for their staff before opening, they share your opening announcement with their customer list.

Launch Window (30 Days Before to Opening Day)

The weeks immediately before and around opening are the highest-leverage marketing moment of year one. Use them well.

Press outreach. Send a one-page press release to local food writers, neighborhood news sites, and the community reporter at your local paper. Include high-quality photos of the space, the team, the coffee. Follow up once, politely.

Storefront as advertisement. Get exterior signage up as early as permits allow. A visible "Opening Soon" sign does daily marketing.

Soft open to email list only. The week or two before grand open, invite your email list to a quiet preview period. They feel like insiders; you get operational debug under lower pressure.

Grand opening event. A specific day with a specific offer (free drink with purchase, opening-week merch, local partnership stamp card). See our grand opening post for the full playbook.

The 90-Day Post-Launch Campaign Cadence

After opening, settle into a consistent monthly cadence that you can maintain forever.

Weekly (every week):

• 3-4 Instagram posts (mix of feed + stories)

• 1-2 stories per day during opening weeks, 3-5 per week after

• Respond to every Google review (positive + negative) within 48 hours

• Respond to DMs and comments within 24 hours

Every 2 weeks:

• Email newsletter to your list (new menu items, seasonal updates, behind-the-scenes, customer spotlights)

• Post 2-3 new photos to Google My Business

Monthly:

• One neighborhood partnership activation (joint event, cross-promotion, collaboration drink)

• Review marketing metrics: email list growth, Instagram follower growth, Google reviews, foot traffic trends

Quarterly:

• Larger event or activation (launch party for a seasonal menu, a free public tasting, a partnership with a local artist or cause)

• Strategic review of what's working and what to change

Instagram: The Daily Discipline

Instagram is the single most important public marketing channel for an independent coffee shop. The baseline:

Content mix: 40% product (drinks, pastries, menu items), 30% behind-the-scenes (you, the team, the process), 20% community (customers, events, partnerships), 10% educational/brand (coffee origin stories, tasting notes, brewing tips).

Photography standards: natural light when possible, consistent editing style, attention to composition. Your Instagram aesthetic should feel cohesive when viewed as a grid — not a random collection of snapshots.

Captions: 1-3 sentences, written in your brand voice, with a small ask or reason to engage. "New seasonal drink this week — pour-over bar tomorrow morning, 7-10. Come taste." A caption with a reason to show up outperforms a caption that just describes the photo.

Hashtags: 5-15 relevant hashtags per post. Mix local (#yourcityfood, #yourneighborhood) with specialty (#specialtycoffee, #coffeegram, #espresso) and brand-specific (your own hashtag if you have one). Hashtags matter less in 2026 than in 2020, but still drive discoverability.

Reels and short-form video: Invest time here. Reels consistently outperform static posts in reach by 2-5x in 2026. A simple 15-second video of pulling a shot or pouring latte art beats a beautifully-shot static photo on discoverability.

Stories: Lower-pressure, daily presence. Behind-the-scenes, prep shots, today's special, quick polls. Stories keep you top-of-mind without requiring every post to be polished.

Email: The Highest-ROI Channel

Email marketing consistently produces the highest return on any dollar or minute spent — typically $30-$50 per dollar invested at healthy open and click rates. It's also the channel most independents neglect.

Build the list aggressively. Every customer visit is a list-building opportunity. Tactics: a small card at the register asking for email in exchange for an occasional 10% off, a QR code on receipts linking to a sign-up form, an opt-in prompt on the Wi-Fi login page if you offer free Wi-Fi.

Send bi-weekly. Content: 3-4 sections maximum per email. Main story (a new drink, a behind-the-scenes, a partnership). Secondary sections (quick news, a featured pastry, a customer spotlight). Clear call-to-action at the bottom (visit today, come to the event, sign up for the loyalty program).

Target open rates: 30%+ for an engaged local list. Click rates: 3%+. Unsubscribe rates under 0.5% per email.

Google My Business: The Underused Channel

"Coffee shops near me" is searched millions of times daily. The shops that appear in the top 3 local results capture disproportionate traffic. Three decisions move Google ranking:

Review quality and quantity. 50+ reviews at 4.7+ stars is the specialty coffee sweet spot. Ask every satisfied customer. Respond to every review within 48 hours.

Fresh photos. Add 2-3 photos per week to your Google profile. Photos of drinks, the space, the team, seasonal changes.

Consistent posting. Google My Business lets you post updates (like mini blog posts). Post weekly: new drinks, hours changes, events, seasonal menus. Google rewards active profiles with better rankings.

These three practices, done consistently, move ranking more than paid ads ever will for most independents.

Word of Mouth: Feed the Flywheel

Word of mouth is organic but not accidental. Practices that accelerate it:

Name customers. Learning regulars' names is the single most powerful retention tool. Regulars tell friends about the shop where the barista knows their order.

Over-deliver on small moments. A complimentary extra shot on a regular's 10th visit. A house-made cookie on a tough-weather Tuesday. A quick thank-you note to a customer who left a thoughtful review. These moments get told and retold.

Referral reward. A simple "bring a friend, both get 20% off" card given to regulars converts some of them into active advocates. Keeps the shop top-of-mind when they're chatting with friends.

What NOT to Spend Money On

Common marketing mistakes new owners make:

Broad Facebook or Instagram ads. Without a specific geographic + interest target, ad spend goes to the wrong audience. Paid ads can work for coffee shops, but only with precise local targeting and a tight offer.

Flyers and print advertising. Generally low ROI in 2026. Occasional tactical use in a dense neighborhood (a doorhanger campaign in a specific 6-block zone) can work, but monthly print spend rarely does.

Radio spots. Expensive, hard to measure, and rarely pay off for a single-location shop.

Stunt marketing. A viral moment once a year is worth far less than steady monthly presence. Don't chase virality; build consistency.

A Marketing Plan You'll Actually Execute

The best marketing plan is one you actually do. Pick a cadence you can maintain — not the one that sounds ambitious on paper. 2 Instagram posts a week that happen beats 5 posts a week that don't. A bi-weekly email that goes out on schedule beats a weekly one you skip half the time. Start with the minimum viable plan, hit it for 90 days, then add. A shop that's consistent on the basics outpaces a shop that's clever on the exotics. Build the marketing machine once and run it every week. By year two, your brand does more work for you than your ads ever could.
