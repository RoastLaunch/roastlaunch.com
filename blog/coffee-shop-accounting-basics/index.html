Coffee Shop Accounting Basics: The Numbers You Actually Have to Track

You don't need a CPA degree. But if you can't read your own P&L, you can't run your shop. Here's the clean, practical version every first-time owner needs.

You didn't get into coffee because you love accounting. Fair. But here's the uncomfortable reality: the owners who survive year one read their financials weekly, not quarterly. They know their labor-to-revenue ratio on Tuesday, not in April when their accountant calls. And they understand their own chart of accounts well enough to spot problems before those problems compound into closures.

This guide covers what a new coffee shop owner actually needs to know about accounting — in plain English, without the CPA-exam fluff. The goal isn't to turn you into a bookkeeper. It's to make sure you can read your own business and know when something is wrong.

The Chart of Accounts: Your Financial Skeleton

A chart of accounts is the list of categories you sort every dollar into. Get this right on day one and every report afterward makes sense. Get it wrong and you'll spend years looking at numbers that don't tell you anything.

A clean specialty coffee shop chart of accounts has roughly 35 to 50 accounts organized into five groups. Revenue accounts: espresso beverages, filter coffee, retail bag sales, food, merchandise, catering, tips-collected (tracked as a pass-through, not revenue). COGS accounts: coffee beans, milk and alternatives, food cost, paper and packaging, retail bag COGS. Operating expenses: rent, CAM/NNN pass-throughs, payroll (wages), payroll taxes, workers' comp, benefits, utilities-electric, utilities-gas, utilities-water, internet, POS subscription, software subscriptions, insurance, marketing, supplies, repairs-maintenance, equipment-lease, professional-services, licenses-permits, bank-fees, credit-card-processing, music-licensing, loan-interest. Assets: cash operating, cash reserves, inventory, prepaid expenses, equipment, leasehold improvements, deposits. Liabilities and equity: accounts-payable, accrued-payroll, sales-tax-payable, credit-cards, SBA-loan, owner-equity, retained-earnings.

Separate COGS accounts by category (beans vs. milk vs. food) rather than lumping all COGS together. That's the difference between "my COGS drifted to 34 percent this month" and "milk is up 18 percent because my oat supplier raised prices and I didn't update menu pricing."

Cash vs. Accrual: Pick Accrual

Cash accounting records revenue when you receive it and expenses when you pay them. Accrual accounting records revenue when it's earned and expenses when they're incurred, regardless of when cash moves. The IRS allows most coffee shops under a certain revenue threshold to file on cash basis.

Use accrual anyway. Accrual shows you the real economics of the business — that January's sales were partly paid for by December's inventory purchase, that your rent is an expense even though you paid the year in advance, that your credit card processing fees should be matched against the sales that produced them. Cash-basis reports can make a losing month look profitable (or vice versa) just because of payment timing. Accrual tells you the truth. Most bookkeeping software (QuickBooks Online, Xero, Wave) runs accrual by default.

The Five Reports You Actually Have to Read

Weekly, and by Thursday of the following week at the latest, you need to be looking at five reports. If you can't read them, your bookkeeper or accountant hasn't set your books up correctly. Ask them to walk you through each one.

Profit and Loss (P&L). Revenue, COGS, operating expenses, net income. Look at both the current week and month-to-date vs. the prior month and vs. your budget. Flag any single line item that moved more than 10 percent without explanation.

Balance Sheet. Your assets, liabilities, and equity as of a specific date. Watch cash and inventory especially. Cash trending down for three weeks in a row without a planned reason is an early-warning signal.

Cash Flow Statement. The timing of money in and out. A coffee shop can show a profit and still run out of cash. The cash flow statement shows you when.

Sales by Category. From your POS system: revenue broken out by espresso, drip, retail bags, food, catering. Percentages should be roughly stable week to week. Sudden shifts (food dropping from 22 percent of revenue to 14 percent) are operational signals — maybe your pastry case is understocked, maybe your new menu item didn't land.

Labor Report. Total labor hours, labor cost, labor as percentage of revenue, by shift or by day. This is the single highest-leverage weekly metric. Hold labor at or below your target percentage, or you'll lose margin you can't recover.

Sales Tax: Collect It, Segregate It, Pay It On Time

Sales tax on prepared food and beverages varies by state and locality — typical combined rates in the U.S. range from 4 to 10.5 percent. Your POS should calculate and collect sales tax on every transaction automatically.

Three rules. First, sales tax is not your money. You're collecting it from customers on behalf of the state. Treat it as a liability from the moment it hits your cash. Second, segregate the funds — a separate bank sub-account dedicated to sales tax prevents you from accidentally using it for operations. Third, file and remit on time, every time. Penalties for late sales tax filing range from 5 to 25 percent plus interest, and several states revoke business licenses for repeat delinquency. Most states require monthly filing for shops above a revenue threshold.

Payroll Taxes: Also Not Your Money

Payroll taxes are the federal and state taxes withheld from employee paychecks (income tax, Social Security, Medicare), plus the employer-side match on Social Security and Medicare (7.65 percent of gross payroll), plus federal and state unemployment (varies by state, typically 2 to 6 percent of the first $7,000 of each employee's wages), plus workers' comp premium.

A payroll processor — Gusto, ADP, Paychex, or your POS's built-in processor — is worth the $40 to $150 per month every time. The processor calculates, withholds, files, and remits on your behalf. Manual payroll processing for even a two-person shop consistently produces late filings, missed deposits, and penalty assessments. The IRS trust-fund recovery penalty for unpaid payroll taxes can be applied personally against the business owner — a pain point no shop should ever reach.

Tips: A Category Unto Themselves

Tips are reportable income to the employee and must be processed through payroll so appropriate taxes are withheld. In 2026, the federal treatment of tip income is evolving — some shops are taking advantage of recent tax changes affecting tip reporting. Consult your accountant for current treatment.

Two operational rules matter regardless of tax changes: (1) pool and distribute tips according to a documented, consistent policy (either equal-pool, hours-based, or house-kept-tip-share), and (2) run tips through payroll on every pay period, not as side cash. Shops that treat tips as informal cash create payroll tax exposure and labor-law liability.

Inventory Accounting: The Monthly Count

Actual COGS on your P&L is calculated with the formula: beginning inventory + purchases − ending inventory. Which means your COGS percentage is only as accurate as your inventory count. Most shops count inventory monthly, at end of month, after close.

Count beans by weight (not by bag), milk by number of gallons on hand, syrups by bottle, cups and lids by sleeve. Enter ending inventory totals into your bookkeeping software. If your COGS percentage jumps by 2 percentage points month over month without a price change, the first suspect is a miscount the prior month; the second is waste, theft, or over-portioning. All three are fixable once you can see them.

Separation of Business and Personal Finances

This is the most common rookie mistake and the one that causes the most legal and tax damage. Every business transaction — income and expense — must flow through a business bank account and business credit card, never personal. Commingling funds weakens the liability protection your LLC or corporation provides (a court can "pierce the corporate veil" and hold you personally liable) and makes tax filing a nightmare.

Day one: open a business checking account, a business savings account, and a business credit card. Route all revenue to the checking account. Pay all expenses from the checking or credit card. Pay yourself via an owner draw (LLC) or W-2 payroll (S-corp) on a regular schedule. Never swipe the business card for personal purchases. Never deposit business cash to a personal account. Ever.

The Bookkeeper and Accountant Question

Two different roles. A bookkeeper records transactions, reconciles accounts, runs payroll, and produces monthly reports. An accountant reviews those reports, advises on tax strategy, files your tax returns, and helps with year-end planning.

Most shops hire a bookkeeper at $200 to $800 per month (depending on volume and whether the work is in-house or outsourced) and an accountant for quarterly check-ins plus annual tax filing, typically $1,200 to $4,000 per year. You still need to read your own reports. The bookkeeper keeps the books clean. You run the business from them.

The One-Hour-a-Week Habit

Block one hour on your calendar every week, same day, same time, to review your financials. Year one this feels like an interruption. By year two it's the most productive hour of your week. That hour is when you see labor drifting up, a COGS category drifting out of spec, cash trending down, or a marketing campaign actually converting.

Accounting isn't about knowing every line of tax code. It's about knowing your business well enough that nothing surprises you — and catching the small problems early, before they become the problems that close coffee shops.
