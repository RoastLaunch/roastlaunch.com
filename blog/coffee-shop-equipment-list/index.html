Coffee Shop Equipment List: Everything You Need to Open in 2026

The full equipment stack — espresso, grinders, brewers, refrigeration, smallwares — with 2026 pricing and the choices that actually matter.

Every new coffee shop owner builds an equipment list at some point. Most start with the espresso machine — then realize three months later they forgot the water softener, the knock box, and the under-counter reach-in. A missed piece of equipment at open can cost you a day, a week, or an entire revenue category. This is the practical, complete equipment list for a specialty coffee shop in 2026, with 2026 pricing, the brands that survive commercial duty, and the decisions that actually move the needle on quality and reliability.

The list is organized by zone: the espresso bar, the brew bar, refrigeration, food prep, the back bar, dishwashing, and smallwares. Every shop is different, but a complete list for a 1,000-square-foot independent with one espresso bar typically totals $45,000 to $85,000.

The Espresso Bar: $15,000 to $45,000

The espresso machine is the centerpiece and the biggest single line item. A two-group commercial machine handles up to about 300 espresso-based drinks per day; a three-group handles up to 600+. Most independents start with a two-group.

Workhorse two-group machines ($8,000-$14,000): Nuova Simonelli Appia Life, Sanremo Zoe, La Spaziale S50, Rancilio Classe 7. These are reliable, serviceable, and make consistently excellent espresso when paired with a quality grinder. Most first-year independent shops should start here.

Premium flow-control two-group ($15,000-$22,000): La Marzocco Linea Mini Classica, Victoria Arduino White Eagle, Sanremo You, Slayer Single Group (multi-group variants). Flow control gives baristas more extraction control and can elevate espresso quality, but only if the team is trained to use it. For most new shops, the workhorse tier is a better value.

Flagship machines ($25,000-$40,000+): La Marzocco KB90 three-group, Slayer Steam EP, Synesso MVP Hydra. Beautiful, excellent, and most justified in mature shops doing 500+ drinks per day with a trained team.

Espresso grinder ($2,000-$5,000): Mahlkönig E65S or E80 Supreme, Mazzer Robur S, Victoria Arduino Mythos 2. The grinder matters as much as the machine — arguably more. Pair a $10,000 machine with a $3,000 grinder rather than a $14,000 machine with a $1,500 grinder.

Water filtration ($500-$2,500): Every coffee machine requires properly treated water. Required in most lease situations and required by manufacturer warranties. Options include Optipure, Everpure, and 3M systems. Test your tap water hardness first; hard water without proper treatment will destroy the boiler.

Espresso accessories ($500-$1,500): Knock box, tamper, distribution tool, precision baskets, cleaning brushes, backflush detergent (Cafiza), milk pitchers (4 to 8 in a rotation), thermometers, scales (a timer scale at each group is now standard).

The Brew Bar: $3,000 to $12,000

Batch brewer ($1,200-$4,000): Marco MIX series, Curtis G4, Fetco CBS series, Bunn ITCB. Batch brewing is higher-margin and faster than single-cup pour-over for most shops. A three-burner station with three airpots or satellite servers handles volume without losing freshness.

Filter grinder ($1,200-$3,500): A separate grinder for batch brew coffee, distinct from the espresso grinder. Mahlkönig EK43S is the gold standard ($3,200 in 2026), but Mahlkönig Guatemala ($1,400) or Ditting 807 handle most shops' needs well.

Pour-over station ($200-$1,000): If you offer pour-over, you need a gooseneck kettle (Fellow Stagg EKG or Bonavita are industry standards), a pour-over scale, filters, and 2 to 4 V60s or Kalita Waves. Low cost, high perceived value, and a signature of specialty shops.

Cold brew setup ($200-$1,500): Toddy commercial system or a dedicated cold brew cart. Kegs and nitro taps add $500 to $2,000 depending on draft tower complexity. Cold brew is high-margin; nitro cold brew can be a menu anchor.

Refrigeration: $5,000 to $15,000

Undercounter reach-in ($2,500-$5,000): Behind the espresso bar for milk. True, Turbo Air, or Beverage-Air are reliable commercial brands. Size for 3 to 5 days of milk supply based on volume.

Back-of-house reach-in ($3,000-$7,000): A full-size two-door refrigerator for bulk storage. Plan 24 to 48 cubic feet for a shop doing $400K annually.

Display case ($2,500-$8,000): Refrigerated or non-refrigerated depending on pastry program. Curved-glass cases look beautiful; straight-glass cases hold more product and are easier to clean. Non-refrigerated is fine for muffins and scones; cream cheese and yogurt parfaits require refrigerated.

Ice machine ($1,500-$4,000): A 300-pound per day unit is sufficient for most shops. Scotsman, Manitowoc, and Hoshizaki are the industry standards. Undercounter units can suffice in low-volume shops, but most shops should plan a head-plus-bin configuration.

Freezer ($1,500-$3,500): Required if you carry ice cream, frozen pastries, or frozen food items.

Food Prep and Back Bar: $3,000 to $10,000

If you serve breakfast or lunch beyond pre-made pastries, you need prep capability. Panini press or sandwich press ($500-$1,500) for hot sandwiches. Convection oven or countertop oven ($800-$3,000) for warming or baking off partially baked goods. Microwave ($200-$800) — commercial grade, not residential. Prep tables with undercounter refrigeration ($2,000-$5,000) if doing any real prep. Blender ($300-$1,500) — Vitamix commercial blenders or Blendtec Stealth are the workhorses for frozen drinks and smoothies.

Keep the hot food menu narrow in year one. Every added item is another SKU to order, store, prep, and potentially waste. Pastries from a local bakery, pre-made sandwiches, and simple panini go a long way.

POS and Technology: $1,000 to $4,000 Up Front

POS hardware typically includes a register/iPad, cash drawer, receipt printer, barcode scanner, and payment processor. Modern cloud POS systems (Toast, Square, Clover, Lightspeed) bundle the hardware with a monthly software fee ($0-$300/month). See the POS Systems post for full detail. Budget $1,000 to $2,500 up front for hardware and expect a monthly software subscription.

Don't forget: Wi-Fi equipment (business-grade router + access points, $300-$800), cable runs ($200-$800), and a small label printer for wholesale bags or sticker labels ($150-$300).

Dishwashing: $2,500 to $8,000

Commercial dishwasher ($2,500-$6,000): Undercounter or pass-through. Low-temp (chemical sanitizer) is cheaper; high-temp (heat sanitizer) is faster and uses no chemicals. CMA and Jackson are common commercial brands.

Three-compartment sink ($800-$2,000): Required by health code in nearly every jurisdiction for proper wash-rinse-sanitize procedures. Dedicated hand-wash sink is also required and separate ($200-$500).

Dish pit accessories ($200-$500): Rack carts, drying racks, dish detergent and sanitizer, scrubbing pads, gloves.

Smallwares, Furniture, and the Line Items That Get Forgotten

The easy-to-overlook stuff, and where first-time owners consistently come up short on budget: cups, lids, sleeves, straws (2-3 weeks of inventory to start, $1,500-$4,000). Milk pitchers in rotation. Tampers and distribution tools. Thermometers. Timers and scales at each station. Cleaning cloths. Mop buckets, brooms, floor mats (a front mat alone runs $150-$300 for quality). Menu boards — printed, digital, or chalkboard ($500-$3,000 for a quality digital setup).

Furniture: tables, chairs, bar stools. Budget $3,000-$15,000 depending on seating count and quality. Chairs are where new owners try to save and regret — a $50 chair that wobbles after six months of commercial use costs more in replacement than a $180 chair that lasts 10 years.

Signage: exterior blade sign, window graphics, menu boards, wayfinding. Total $1,500-$8,000 depending on complexity.

The 10% Contingency Rule

Add 10 percent to whatever your final equipment list totals. You will forget something. You will need an extra piece after a health inspector points out a gap. You'll decide on opening week to add a second grinder or a better blender. A $60,000 equipment budget should assume $66,000 in actual spend by opening day.

New vs. Used: When Used Works and When It Doesn't

Used equipment can cut 30 to 50 percent off the equipment bill — but only if you buy smart. Good candidates for used: refrigeration (commercial-grade, inspected, with service history), prep tables, shelving, smallwares. Bad candidates for used: espresso machines without a certified rebuild, POS systems, water filtration, ice machines. An un-rebuilt used espresso machine often costs more in its first-year repairs than the savings on purchase.

If buying used, buy from reputable restaurant supply dealers or specialty coffee equipment resellers (Prima Coffee, Espresso Parts, Seattle Coffee Gear often resell certified-rebuilt machines). Avoid Craigslist and private sales on major equipment.

Build the List Before You Build the Shop

A complete equipment list serves two functions: it's your budget for the biggest line item in your startup, and it's the spec sheet your architect or designer uses to lay out the bar. Build the list first. Price it with an actual dealer (not MSRP from manufacturer sites). Lay out the bar to fit the equipment rather than forcing equipment into a bar that's already designed. The shops that open smoothly are the ones where the espresso machine, the grinder, the undercounter fridge, and the dishwasher were all on the architectural plans from day one — not shoehorned in three weeks before opening.

Don't Forget the Small Stuff

Owners obsess over the espresso machine and forget the fifty smaller items that make a shop function. A partial list: milk pitchers (3-6 of each size), knock boxes, tamp mats, scales (one per espresso station), thermometers, timers, cleaning brushes, group-head brushes, espresso machine cleaner, grinder brushes, portafilters (extras for peak hours), cup warmers, ice scoops, garnish containers, pastry tongs, squeeze bottles, sauce dispensers, POS receipt paper, label printers, trash cans, floor mats, cleaning chemicals, towels (shop towels, bar towels, dish towels), first aid kit, and the dozen small fixtures and fittings that bars consume constantly. Budget $3,000-$6,000 for small-wares. Make a detailed list; your equipment dealer can help source most of it.
